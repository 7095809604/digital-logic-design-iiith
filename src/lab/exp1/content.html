Adder Circuit
Introduction

This module explains how to build a adder circuit.In electronics, an adder or summer is a digital circuit that performs addition of numbers. In many computers and other kinds of processors, adders are used not only in the arithmetic logic unit(s), but also in other parts of the processor, where they are used to calculate addresses, table indices, and similar.

Theory

Half Adder

A half adder adds two one-bit binary numbers A and B to generate a 1-bit SUM(S) and a 1-bit CARRY(C) as the output.(the value theoretically carried on to the next addition); the final sum is 2C + S. The simplest half-adder design, pictured on the right, incorporates an XOR gate for S and an AND gate for C. Half adders cannot be used compositely, given their incapacity for a carry-in bit. The outputs S and C can be expressed as logical functions of input variables A,B as follows :
SUM = 1 either if (A = 0 and B = 1) or if (A = 1 and B = 0).
CARRY = 1 only if (A = 1 and B = 1)

Full Adder

A full adder adds binary numbers and accounts for values carried in as well as out. A one-bit full adder adds three one-bit numbers, often written as A, B, and Cin; A and B are the operands, and Cin is a bit carried in (in theory from a past addition). The circuit produces a two-bit output sum typically represented by the signals Cout and S, where Sum=2 X Cout + S . A full adder can be implemented in many different ways such as with a custom transistor-level circuit or composed of other gates. One example implementation is with S = A exor B exor Cin and Cout = (A.B)+(Cin.(A exor B))

Ripple Carry Adder

Multiple full adders can be used to create a N-bit adder. Each full adder inputs a Cin, which is the Cout of the previous adder. This kind of adder is a ripple carry adder, since each carry bit "ripples" to the next full adder. Note that the first (and only the first) full adder may be replaced by a half adder.
